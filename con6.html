Delegation in Action

The 12 Concepts Illustrated
"We have seen that the 'final responsibility and ultimate authority' for A.A.'s service activities rest with the A.A. groups (Concept I), but to carry out this responsibility they must delegate to the Conference (Concept II). The Conference, in turn, must delegate administrative authority to the General Service Board of Trustees. Again, it is helpful if you are familiar with both the Conference Charter and the Bylaws of the General Service Board to understand this relationship and the freedom of action that the trustees must have."
Key takeaway

Concept VI establishes that just as groups delegate active authority to the Conference, the Conference delegates liberal administrative authority to the General Service Board, allowing the Trustees to act freely and decisively when the annual Conference is not in session.

The Trustees & Core Papers

The General Service Board (G.S.B.) coordinates world services using a unique blend of professional and recovering leadership.

Class A Trustees (Nonalcoholic)

Nonalcoholic professionals (law, finance, public relations, medicine). They carry no stigma, face no anonymity barriers at the public level, and supply objective leadership.

Class B Trustees (Alcoholic)

Alcoholic members with deep experience in local recovery and service structure. They carry the spiritual heart of the AA program into board deliberations.

Historical Context: Bill W.'s Original 1962 Writings

Reprinted from the April 1972 AA Grapevine Essay

The "Point of Safe Return" Principle

Bill W. noted that our world service relationships are based on a vast amount of hard-won experience. He explicitly stated that we must put these relationships into writing so that we are not tempted into hasty, unwise amendments due to a temporary lack of understanding.

"Even if we do some day make changes that happen to work out poorly, then the experience of the past will not have been lost. These articles can then be relied upon as a point of safe return."

No "Freezing" Into Rigid Patterns

Bill recognized that future generations would uncover flaws or confront new conditions requiring major refinements. Thus, he insisted the Conference Charter remain readily amendable, warning against freezing our administrative design into a rigid, unyielding mold.

The 1960 Historical Financial Scale

To understand why Bill insisted the G.S.B. function like a corporate board, consider the historical size of the operations. In 1960, the combined gross receipts of A.A. World Services, Inc. and the A.A. Grapevine, Inc. were already approaching $500,000 annually (nearly half a million dollars).

The G.S.B. was also responsible for investing and protecting the substantial **Reserve Fund**. Managing such an operation required highly specialized skills:

  • Specific professional & legal skills.
  • Advanced investment and financial planning.
  • Coordination of 5 (now 7) G.S.B. standing committees.

The Ultimate Analogy

The Corporate Model vs. Institutions

Bill W. deliberately chose the corporate vehicle instead of the governmental or institutional structure because of its superior agility.

Stockholders

The collective **A.A. Groups** act as the ultimate owners and stockholders of the entire world service structure, representing ultimate voice.

Proxy Holders

The **Area Delegates** represent the groups as proxy-holders at the Annual Meeting (the General Service Conference), setting policy.

Holding Company

The **General Service Board** functions as a holding company, composed of trustees who have full legal power to manage funds.

Active Subsidiaries

**A.A.W.S.** and **A.A. Grapevine** operate as active subsidiary corporations, processing publishing and coordination tasks independently.

Why did we avoid the government model? Bill wrote that administrative freedom in a business structure outperforms top-heavy government bureaucracy. The GSB requires large executive powers to remain functional and fast-moving.

Deep Dive Terminology

Chief Initiative

Chief Initiative represents the proactive mandate of the GSB. Because the Conference meets only once per year, the Trustees are expected to seek out problems and plan business solutions without waiting for an annual directive.

How this works in world service:

  • Tracking multi-million dollar cash flows to address shortfalls.
  • Proposing technical and operational updates to the General Service Office.
  • Researching literature distribution issues before delegates arrive.
Deep Dive Terminology

Active Responsibility

Active Responsibility denotes executive execution and legal accountability. If a corporate or administrative action fails, the G.S.B. holds the primary liability, shielding the individual delegates.

The Trustees' functional liabilities:

  • Signing office leases and legal publishing contracts.
  • Managing GSO employees and administering payroll.
  • Safeguarding our reserve funds and managing tax compliance.

The Spiritual Essence of Concept VI

Trust and Freedom: This concept warns against micromanagement. We select qualified trusted servants, equip them with adequate boundaries, and then trust them to serve with autonomy. If they fail, we replace them; but while they serve, we grant them structural support.
