## Slide 1 — Title: Humility

> Welcome, everyone. This month in our 36 Principles study we're on **Month 7**, and the principle that ties this month together is **humility**.
>
> The way this format works — for anyone new to it — is that we take the Step, the Tradition, and the Concept that share the *same number* and we read them side by side. The idea is that A.A.'s three legacies — Recovery, Unity, and Service — aren't three separate programs. They're three expressions of the same spiritual life. So tonight we're holding **Step 7, Tradition 7, and Concept 7** together, and asking: what's the one principle running through all three?
>
> The answer is humility — at the individual level, at the group level, and at the level of A.A.'s whole service structure.

## Slide 4 — Humility is not humiliation

> Before we go further, we have to deal with the word itself, because **humility is probably the most misunderstood word in all of recovery.**
>
> Humility is **not** humiliation. It's not self-abasement. It's not thinking poorly of yourself, and it's not weakness or groveling.

*[Gesture to the right panel.]*

> The Twelve and Twelve gives us the real definition. Humility is *"a clear recognition of what and who we really are, followed by a sincere attempt to become what we could be."*
>
> Notice — there's nothing self-hating in that. It's about *seeing clearly*, and then *growing.*

*[Advance the fragment.]*

> And at every single level, humility produces the same result: **freedom** — freedom from the trap of self-sufficiency, self-importance, and the illusion that power is the answer.

## Slide 5 — Short form / long form

> If I had to put the shared principle in one short line, it's this:
>
> **"We ask rather than demand, cooperate rather than compel, and pay our own way."**
>
> And that one line plays out as the same act at three levels. In **Step 7**, I approach my Higher Power as a *supplicant — not a commander.* In **Tradition 7**, the group practices humility before the world: *"we'll pay our own way, and we will not accept the strings that come attached to outside money."* And in **Concept 7**, we get institutional humility — a Charter that runs on *voluntary compliance, not legal force* — a service structure that deliberately refuses to accumulate power.

*[Advance the disclaimer fragment. Read it plainly — it matters.]*

It's not official A.A. language. The Steps, the Traditions, and the Concepts belong to A.A.; the connections I'm drawing between them are mine. Take what's useful and leave the rest.

## Slide 6 — The Step: Humility before God

> Let's take each level in turn, starting with the **individual — Step 7.**
>
> *"Humbly asked Him to remove our shortcomings."* The key word is **"humbly."** In fact, the entire chapter on Step 7 in the Twelve and Twelve is really a chapter about humility.
>
> What Step 7 asks of me is humility before God: **asking** for help rather than **demanding** results. It's accepting that I cannot remove my own shortcomings through willpower alone — that white-knuckling my character defects has never worked.

*[Read the Seventh Step Prayer — slow down here.]*

> And the form that takes is the Seventh Step Prayer from page 76 of the Big Book:
>
> *"My Creator, I am now willing that you should have all of me, good and bad. I pray that you now remove from me every single defect of character which stands in the way of my usefulness to you and my fellows. Grant me strength, as I go out from here, to do your bidding. Amen."*

---

## Slide 7 — The Tradition: Self-Supporting

> Now move up a level — from the individual to the **group. Tradition 7.**
>
> This is humility applied to money. *"Every A.A. group ought to be fully self-supporting, declining outside contributions."*
>
> Here's the thing I want to stress: **this is not about poverty. It's about freedom.** In the early days, A.A. accepted gifts from wealthy benefactors, and it learned the hard way that money brings strings — and strings compromise independence. So by declining outside money, the group humbly says: *we will pay our own way.*

*[Read the quote.]*

> The Twelve and Twelve puts it bluntly: *"The A.A. groups themselves ought to be fully self-supporting, declining outside contributions... Nothing can so surely destroy our spiritual heritage as futile disputes over property, money, and authority."*

---

## Slide 8 — The Concept: The Charter

> And now the third level — the whole **service structure. Concept 7.**
>
> Concept 7 is about the Conference Charter and Bylaws. But the insight I want you to catch is this: the Charter relies on **voluntary compliance, not legal compulsion.**
>
> The Conference is what Bill called a "creature of the Fellowship." It has *no power* to compel any group to do anything. Its authority rests entirely on moral persuasion and the trust of the groups. Think about that — here's an organization that could have built in legal enforcement, and it deliberately chose not to. That's humility at the institutional level: the refusal to accumulate power.

*[Read the quote.]*

> *"The Conference Charter is not a legal document; it is more in the nature of a traditional agreement... Its real force lies not in law but in the voluntary compliance of A.A.'s membership."*

---

## Slide 9 — The matrix: one principle, three dimensions

> Let's pull it all together on one screen. Same principle — humility — read across all three levels.
>
> Look at **what humility looks like** in each column: at the individual level, asking God rather than demanding. At the group level, declining outside money. At the service level, leaning on moral authority instead of legal force.
>
> Now look at the row I really want you to sit with — **the paradox.** Admitting weakness gives me strength. Declining money gives the group freedom. And having no power to enforce actually gives the service structure its credibility. Every level contains a truth that's completely backwards from how the world thinks.
>
> And notice the bottom row — **the spiritual act** is the same all the way down: humbly asking, humbly paying our own way, humbly operating by consent.

## Slide 10 — The cross-connection

> So here's the thread. *Humility* is the thread.

*[Walk the flow left to right.]*

> **I** humbly ask God. The **group** humbly declines outside money. The **Conference** humbly relies on moral authority. Same act, three levels.
>
> Step back and look at what the world runs on — **power, money, and legal authority.** A.A. deliberately rejects all three, and replaces them with something quieter: humility, self-support, and voluntary cooperation. Step 7: ask rather than demand. Tradition 7: pay our own way rather than take the strings. Concept 7: rely on consent rather than compulsion.

*[Advance the closing fragment.]*

> Which is why I'd argue this is **perhaps the most counter-cultural principle in all of A.A.**

## Slide 11 — Historical context: the $10,000 bequest

> Where did this come from? There's a story behind Tradition 7.
>
> In A.A.'s early years, the Fellowship was left a bequest — **ten thousand dollars** — from a grateful member. And Bill W. and the early trustees genuinely argued about it. One side said: *A.A. needs money, we should gratefully accept this.* The other side said: *if we start taking large bequests, we're setting a dangerous precedent.*
>
> And the decision they landed on — to **limit the size of individual bequests** and to **cap the size of A.A.'s reserve fund** — was an act of institutional humility. That decision became the foundation of Tradition 7.

*[Gesture to the gold panel.]*

> And look how cleanly it parallels the other two levels: just as I decline to rely on my own willpower and instead ask God — A.A. declines outside wealth and instead asks its own members — and the Conference declines legal enforcement and instead operates by consent. The same humility, in three different rooms.

## Slide 13 — What happens when the balance breaks

> Now — because we're studying all three together — it's worth asking: what happens when humility is strong at one level but weak at another?

*[Card 1.]*

> **Strong Step, weak Tradition:** I pray the Seventh Step Prayer every day, I practice personal humility — but my group accepts large outside donations, or it survives because one wealthy member keeps it afloat. My personal humility sits right next to institutional pride, and the group ends up compromised and beholden to its donors.

*[Card 2.]*

> **Strong Tradition, weak Step:** the group is beautifully self-supporting, declines every outside dollar — but the members never did the personal work of Step 7. They're proud, self-righteous, sure of their own importance. The group's financial humility is masking an arrogance of spirit, and newcomers can *feel* it.

*[Card 3.]*

> **Strong Step and Tradition, weak Concept:** personal humility and group self-support are both healthy — but the service structure starts trying to *compel.* Committees overstep, districts try to dictate to groups, rules and mandates and threats creep in. Result: groups disengage, and the whole structure loses credibility.

*[Advance the fragment.]*

> The lesson: humility has to be lived at **all three levels at once.** Weakness anywhere undermines the whole.

## Slide 14 — Meditation for the month

> Let me read the meditation for this month. If it feels right, let's take it as a quiet moment together.

*[Slow down. Read gently.]*

> *"God, I come to You humbly — not because I am worthless, but because I am not God. I cannot remove my own shortcomings, and I stop pretending I can. I ask for Your help. May my group approach its finances with the same humility — paying its own way, declining what comes with strings, trusting that self-support is a form of spiritual health. And may our service structure always choose persuasion over power, consent over compulsion, and moral authority over legal force. Humility is not thinking less of myself — it is thinking of myself less. Help me practice that today, in my recovery, in my group, and in my service."*
