Defining the Word

Humility Is Not Humiliation

What it is not

  • Humiliation or self-abasement
  • Thinking poorly of oneself
  • Weakness or groveling

What it is (12&12)

“A clear recognition of what and who we really are, followed by a sincere attempt to become what we could be.” — 12&12, on Step 7

The result at every level: freedom from the trap of self-sufficiency, self-importance, and the illusion that power is the answer.

Humility is the most misunderstood word in recovery. Clear the misconception first, then give the 12&12 definition. See remarks, Slide 4.

The Shared Principle · Humility

Short Form

“We ask rather than demand, cooperate rather than compel, and pay our own way.”

Long form — the same act at three levels: Step 7 approaches the Higher Power as a supplicant, not a commander. Tradition 7 is humility before the world — “we will pay our own way; we will not accept the strings that come with outside money.” Concept 7 is institutional humility — a Charter that relies on voluntary compliance, not legal compulsion; a service structure that refuses to accumulate power.

Note: the shared principle, short form, and long form reflect a personal understanding of how the three legacies connect. They are not official A.A. language. The Steps, Traditions, and Concepts are A.A.’s; the connections drawn between them are the author’s own.

Read the short form aloud, then unpack the three clauses. End by reading the disclaimer so the room knows what's official and what's interpretation. See remarks, Slide 5.

Individual · Recovery

The Step — Humility before God 12&12 pp.70–76

Step 7 humbly asks God to remove my shortcomings. The key word is “humbly.” It means asking for help rather than demanding results — recognizing I cannot remove my own shortcomings through willpower alone.

“My Creator, I am now willing that you should have all of me, good and bad. I pray that you now remove from me every single defect of character which stands in the way of my usefulness to you and my fellows. Grant me strength, as I go out from here, to do your bidding. Amen.” — Big Book p.76, the Seventh Step Prayer
The Step level: humility before God. The whole 12&12 chapter on Step 7 is really about humility. Read the prayer. See remarks, Slide 6.

Group · Unity

The Tradition — Self-Supporting 12&12 pp.160–165

Humility applied to the group’s finances. This is not about poverty — it is about freedom. Early A.A. learned the hard way that money brings strings, and strings compromise independence. By declining outside money, the group humbly says: we will pay our own way.

“The A.A. groups themselves ought to be fully self-supporting, declining outside contributions… Nothing can so surely destroy our spiritual heritage as futile disputes over property, money, and authority.” — 12&12 pp.160–161
The Group level: humility before the world. Stress "freedom, not poverty." Money brings strings. See remarks, Slide 7.

Service · World Affairs

The Concept — The Charter

Concept 7 describes the Conference Charter and Bylaws — but the key insight is that the Charter relies on voluntary compliance, not legal compulsion. The Conference is a “creature of the Fellowship.” It has no power to compel any group to do anything. Its authority rests entirely on moral persuasion and the trust of the groups.

“The Conference Charter is not a legal document; it is more in the nature of a traditional agreement… Its real force lies not in law but in the voluntary compliance of A.A.’s membership.” — Concept VII (paraphrased)
The Service level: institutional humility. An organization that could compel compliance chooses not to. Authority is moral, not legal. See remarks, Slide 8.

The world runs on power, money, and legal authority. A.A. deliberately rejects all three in favor of something quieter: humility, self-support, and voluntary cooperation.

Step 7: ask rather than demand. Tradition 7: pay our own way rather than accept strings. Concept 7: rely on consent rather than compulsion.

This is perhaps the most counter-cultural principle in all of A.A.

Historical Context

The $10,000 Bequest

In A.A.’s early years, the Fellowship received a bequest of $10,000 from a grateful member. Bill W. and the trustees debated: A.A. needs money — accept it gratefully? Or large bequests set a dangerous precedent?

The decision — to limit individual bequests and cap the size of A.A.’s reserve — was an act of institutional humility that became the foundation of Tradition 7.

The same humility, three places: I decline to rely on my own willpower and ask God (Step 7). A.A. declines outside wealth and asks its own members (Tradition 7). The Conference declines legal enforcement and operates by consent (Concept 7).

Tell the bequest story like a story. It is the concrete origin of Tradition 7 and shows institutional humility in action. See remarks, Slide 11.

Personal Understanding

“Humility was the word I misunderstood the longest. I thought it meant groveling, self-deprecation, thinking I was worthless. The 12&12 taught me otherwise: humility is a clear recognition of what and who I really am.”

When I humbly ask God (Step 7), my group declines outside contributions (Tradition 7), and the Conference relies on moral authority (Concept 7) — each is simply acknowledging reality. Humility is not weakness. It is the most practical and powerful spiritual principle I know.

This is a personal-share slide. Deliver it from the heart — or invite a member to share their own experience of misunderstanding humility. See remarks, Slide 12.

What Happens When the Balance Breaks

Humility Strong at One Level, Weak at Others

Strong Step, Weak Tradition

I pray daily — but the group takes large outside donations or leans on a wealthy member. Personal humility coexists with institutional pride; the group is compromised and beholden to its donors.

Strong Tradition, Weak Step

The group is fully self-supporting — but members never practiced Step 7. Financial humility masks an arrogance of spirit that newcomers can feel.

Strong Step & Tradition, Weak Concept

Personal and group humility are strong — but the service structure tries to compel through mandates and threats. Groups disengage; the structure loses credibility.

Humility has to be lived at all three levels at once — weakness anywhere undermines the whole.

The "failure modes" slide. Each card is a real-world imbalance. Good place to pause for nods of recognition. See remarks, Slide 13.

Meditation for This Month

“God, I come to You humbly — not because I am worthless, but because I am not God. I cannot remove my own shortcomings, and I stop pretending I can. I ask for Your help. May my group approach its finances with the same humility — paying its own way, declining what comes with strings, trusting that self-support is a form of spiritual health. And may our service structure always choose persuasion over power, consent over compulsion, and moral authority over legal force. Humility is not thinking less of myself — it is thinking of myself less. Help me practice that today, in my recovery, in my group, and in my service.” — A meditation on Humility at every level
Slow the room down. Consider reading this as a shared moment of quiet. See remarks, Slide 14.

For Group Discussion

  • Review the group’s finances: self-supporting? Giving beyond rent to district, area, GSO?

  • Journal: where am I substituting power for humility?
