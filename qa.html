How to Use This Guide
  • At your Home Group: Pick 2-3 votes that matter most to your group. Read the questions aloud at a business meeting.
  • At District: Use the visual diagrams to facilitate informed discussion. Each "consideration map" shows why a delegate might vote yes or no.

  • Principle: The delegate votes her informed conscience (Concept III) — but your input IS the conscience she carries (Concept I).

Consideration Map — How Principles Apply
Supports YES

Item 9b PAI 9

Should the Dr. Bob Quote Be Removed from the Service Manual?

1939 quote · "The Service Sponsor" section, p.10 of Service Manual · Dr. Bob died Nov 16 1950 — 5 months before the 1st GSC (April 1951)

Key Context: Individual Submission

PAI 9 was submitted by an individual member, not a group, district, or area. No group conscience stands behind this request. Under Concept I, the Conference must weigh whether an individual proposal to alter a co-founder's legacy carries the same weight as one arising from the collective conscience process.

Consideration Map
Supports REMOVAL
Historical Accuracy: The quote is from 1939. Dr. Bob died Nov 16, 1950 — five months before the first GSC in April 1951. He never saw the service structure he's quoted about.
Tradition 2 (Group Conscience): If the quote discourages service participation, it undermines group conscience at the GSR level
Concept I: The Conference is the voice of the Fellowship — if it decides the quote is misleading in context, removing it honors that authority
Concept VII (Conference Charter): The Service Manual is a Conference-owned document. The Conference has full authority to edit its own materials.
Supports KEEPING
Tradition 1 (Unity): Erasing a co-founder's words from our own service manual threatens the historical unity of A.A.
Tradition 12 (Anonymity): Principles before personalities — but Dr. Bob IS a principle in A.A. history
No Group Conscience: This is an individual submission. No group, district, or area voted to request this change. Under Concept V, even individual voices deserve hearing — but does an individual proposal warrant altering a co-founder's words?
Warranty 5: Removing a co-founder's quote could be perceived as personally punitive toward his legacy.
Alternative: Add context rather than remove. Explain the 1939 date and what the quote meant in its era.
Precedent: Once we start removing co-founder quotes, where does it end?
Questions to Ask Your Delegate
  1. Delegate, this PAI was submitted by an individual member — not a group or area. Under Concept I, should the Conference act on a proposal that didn't emerge from any group conscience process? (Ref: 9b — PAI 9 submission)
  2. Dr. Bob died on November 16, 1950 — five months before the first GSC in April 1951. The 1939 quote in "The Service Sponsor" section (p.10) predates the entire service structure. Does it still serve our members in context, or has it become misleading?
  3. Rather than removal, would you support adding historical context (e.g., a footnote explaining the 1939 date and that Dr. Bob never witnessed the GSC)? Is that a compromise that honors both Tradition 1 and accuracy?
  4. Under Warranty 5, "no Conference action shall ever be personally punitive." Could removing a co-founder's quote be perceived as punitive toward his legacy, even if that's not the intent?
  5. Under Tradition 12, we place principles above personalities. But our co-founders' words carry weight. How do you weigh a co-founder's quote against historical accuracy?
  6. If this quote is removed, does it set a precedent for removing other historical quotes from the Service Manual that some find problematic?
  7. Has your area discussed this, and does the group conscience (Concept I) lean toward removal, context, or keeping it as-is?

Item 8d PI Committee

Should the 2026 Membership Survey Proceed — or Delay for a Pilot?

35% female (probability) vs 52% female (convenience) · Dr. Cooper recommends delay · Unlisted groups problem · $18,500 cost

The Gender Discrepancy Problem

The probability sample (statistically random) shows A.A. is 35% female. The convenience sample (voluntary) shows 52% female. This 17-point gap raises fundamental questions about which number is real — and whether our survey methodology has been valid for decades.

Unlisted groups: Many groups don't register with GSO and can't be sampled. This creates a systematic blind spot in any probability-based survey.

Dr. Cooper's recommendation: Delay the full survey and conduct a methodological pilot first to resolve the discrepancy before publishing potentially misleading data to professionals, the media, and the Fellowship.

Consideration Map
Supports PROCEED Now
Tradition 5 (Primary Purpose): Professionals rely on our survey data to refer people to A.A. Outdated data from 2014 hampers outreach.

Delegate Mechanics — How Voting Actually Works

Understanding the mechanics helps you have a more informed conversation with your delegate. These aren't just procedural details — they shape outcomes.

Voting Thresholds — Simple Majority vs 2/3
Requires 2/3 Majority
  • 5f — GV contributions (supersedes 1994 Advisory Action)
  • 6c — 5th Edition Big Book (new Conference-approved literature)
  • 9b — Dr. Bob quote removal (Service Manual/Charter change)
  • Any change to the Conference Charter
  • Any new Conference-approved literature
Simple Majority
  • Most other items — recommendations, studies, reports
  • 1f — La Vigne presentation (procedural)
  • 4d — GSC location study (study only, not a move)
  • 4c — Step guide (to begin development, not final approval)
  • Committee recommendations and progress reports
Ask: Delegate, for items requiring 2/3 — if the vote is close (e.g., 60-40), will you voice your position as minority opinion (Concept V)? Minority opinions are recorded and can influence future Conferences.
Advisory Actions vs Committee Recommendations
Advisory Action

A formal Conference decision that carries the weight of the group conscience of A.A. as a whole. Published in the Final Report. Becomes policy that future Conferences generally respect (though they can overturn). Advisory Actions are what "sticks."

Committee Recommendation

A suggestion from a Conference committee to the full Conference floor. It becomes an Advisory Action only if approved by the full Conference. Committees can also "take no action," "refer back," or "request more information" — none of which become Advisory Actions.

Ask: Delegate, for items you feel strongly about, will you advocate in committee for a floor action if the committee takes no action? Or respect the committee process under Concept III?
Concept III — The Delegate's Right of Decision

Your delegate has the right to vote her own informed conscience after hearing Conference discussion — even if it differs from the area's instruction. This is not a betrayal of the area; it IS the design of our service structure.

What the area CAN do:
  • Share your group conscience clearly
  • Provide context and reasoning
  • Ask the delegate to explain her vote afterward
  • Express strong feelings on specific items
What the area CANNOT do:
  • Mandate how the delegate votes
  • Recall the delegate for voting differently
  • Treat a different vote as a breach of trust
  • Ignore new information the delegate learned at Conference
Ask: Delegate, on which items do you feel bound by the area's conscience, and on which do you feel you need the freedom to vote differently after Conference discussion? How will you communicate your decisions to us afterward?
Floor Actions — The Votes You Can't Prepare For

Any Conference member can introduce a floor action — a motion not on the original agenda. These are debated and voted on the Conference floor. Floor actions can amend, expand, or introduce entirely new items. Your delegate will face votes you haven't discussed.

Ask: Delegate, when a floor action comes up that our area hasn't discussed, what principles will you use to decide? Can you commit to filtering unexpected motions through the Traditions and Concepts — even under time pressure?
Capturing the "Sense of the Area"

Before Conference, your area assembly should formally capture positions on key items. This gives the delegate a clear record of the area's group conscience to carry — even when she exercises her Right of Decision.

Recommended Process:
  1. Pre-Assembly: Share this Q&A guide with groups 30+ days before assembly. Ask GSRs to bring group conscience.
  2. At Assembly: Workshop the top 5-7 items. Use the Consideration Maps for balanced discussion.
  3. Formal Vote: For each item discussed, take a sense-of-the-area vote: YES / NO / NO INSTRUCTION (delegate decides).
  4. Record: Give the delegate a written summary of each vote with the reasoning discussed.
  5. Follow-Up: After Conference, ask the delegate to report back on how she voted and why — including any floor actions.
What Happens After the Vote
If it PASSES (becomes Advisory Action)
  • Published in the Final Report (typically fall)
  • GSO/AAWS begins implementation
  • Literature projects: 1-3 year development cycle
  • Policy changes: effective immediately
  • Studies: report back to next year's Conference
If it FAILS
  • Item can be resubmitted as a PAI next year
  • Minority opinion is recorded
  • Committee may request further study
  • "No action" ≠ "never" — it means "not now"
  • Standing items automatically return next year
