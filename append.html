II
Spiritual Experience

The terms "spiritual experience" and "spiritual awakening" are used many times in this book which, upon careful reading, shows that the personality change sufficient to bring about recovery from alcoholism has manifested itself among us in many different forms.

Yet it is true that our first printing gave many readers the impression that these personality changes, or religious experiences, must be in the nature of sudden and spectacular upheavals. Happily for everyone, this conclusion is erroneous.

In the first few chapters a number of sudden revolutionary changes are described. Though it was not our intention to create such an impression, many alcoholics have nevertheless concluded that in order to recover they must acquire an immediate and overwhelming "God-consciousness" followed at once by a vast change in feeling and outlook.

Among our rapidly growing membership of thousands of alcoholics such transformations, though frequent, are by no means the rule. Most of our experiences are what the psychologist William James calls the "educational variety" because they develop slowly over a period of time. Quite often friends of the newcomer are aware of the difference long before he is himself. He finally realizes that he has undergone a profound alteration in his reaction to life; that such a change could hardly have been brought about by himself alone. What often takes place in a few months could seldom have been accomplished by years of self-discipline. With few exceptions our members find that they have tapped an unsuspected inner resource which they presently identify with their own conception of a Power greater than themselves.

Most of us think this awareness of a Power greater than ourselves is the essence of spiritual experience. Our more religious members call it "God-consciousness."

Most emphatically we wish to say that any alcoholic capable of honestly facing his problems in the light of our experience can recover, provided he does not close his mind to all spiritual concepts. He can only be defeated by an attitude of intolerance or belligerent denial.

We find that no one need have difficulty with the spirituality of the program. "Willingness, honesty and open mindedness are the essentials of recovery. But these are indispensable."

"There is a principle which is a bar against all information, which is proof against all arguments and which cannot fail to keep a man in everlasting ignorance—that principle is contempt prior to investigation."

III
The Medical View on A.A.

Since Dr. Silkworth's first endorsement of Alcoholics Anonymous, medical societies and physicians throughout the world have set their approval upon us. Following are excerpts from the comments of doctors present at the annual meeting of the Medical Society of the State of New York where a paper on A.A. was read:

Dr. Foster Kennedy, neurologist:

"This organization of Alcoholics Anonymous calls on two of the greatest reservoirs of power known to man, religion and that instinct for association with one's fellows . . . the 'herd instinct.' I think our profession must take appreciative cognizance of this great therapeutic weapon. If we do not do so, we shall stand convicted of emotional sterility and of having lost the faith that moves mountains, without which medicine can do little."

Dr. G. Kirby Collier, psychiatrist:

"I have felt that A.A. is a group unto themselves and their best results can be had under their own guidance, as a result of their philosophy. Any therapeutic or philosophic procedure which can prove a recovery rate of 50% to 60% must merit our consideration."

Dr. Harry M. Tiebout, psychiatrist:

"As a psychiatrist, I have thought a great deal about the relationship of my specialty to A.A. and I have come to the conclusion that our particular function can very often lie in preparing the way for the patient to accept any sort of treatment or outside help. I now conceive the psychiatrist's job to be the task of breaking down the patient's inner resistance so that which is inside him will flower, as under the activity of the A.A. program."

Dr. W. W. Bauer, broadcasting under the auspices of The American Medical Association in 1946, over the NBC network:

"Alcoholics Anonymous are no crusaders; not a temperance society. They know that they must never drink. They help others with similar problems . . . In this atmosphere the alcoholic often overcomes his excessive concentration upon himself. Learning to depend upon a higher power and absorb himself in his work with other alcoholics, he remains sober day by day. The days add up into weeks, the weeks into months and years."

Dr. John F. Stouffer, Chief Psychiatrist, Philadelphia General Hospital:

"The alcoholics we get here at Philadelphia General are mostly those who cannot afford private treatment, and A.A. is by far the greatest thing we have been able to offer them. Even among those who occasionally land back in here again, we observe a profound change in personality. You would hardly recognize them."

The American Psychiatric Association requested, in 1949, that a paper be prepared by one of the older members of Alcoholics Anonymous to be read at the Association's annual meeting of that year. This was done, and the paper was printed in the American Journal of Psychiatry for November 1949.

IV
The Lasker Award

In 1951 the Lasker Award was given Alcoholics Anonymous. The citation reads in part as follows:

"The American Public Health Association presents a Lasker Group Award for 1951 to Alcoholics Anonymous in recognition of its unique and highly successful approach to that age-old public health and social problem, alcoholism . . . In emphasizing alcoholism as an illness, the social stigma associated with this condition is being blotted out . . . Historians may one day recognize Alcoholics Anonymous to have been a great venture in social pioneering which forged a new instrument for social action; a new therapy based on the kinship of common suffering; one having a vast potential for the myriad other ills of mankind."

The Religious View on A.A.

Clergymen of practically every denomination have given A.A. their blessing.

Edward Dowling, S.J.,* of the Queen's Work staff:

"Alcoholics Anonymous is natural; it is natural at the point where nature comes closest to the supernatural, namely in humiliations and in consequent humility. There is something spiritual about an art museum or a symphony, and the Catholic Church approves of our use of them. There is something spiritual about A.A. too, and Catholic participation in it almost invariably results in poor Catholics becoming better Catholics."

The Living Church (Episcopal magazine) observes editorially:

"The basis of the technique of Alcoholics Anonymous is the truly Christian principle that a man cannot help himself except by helping others. The A.A. plan is described by the members themselves as 'self-insurance.' This self-insurance has resulted in the restoration of physical, mental and spiritual health and self-respect to hundreds of men and women who would be hopelessly down and out without its unique but effective therapy."

Dr. Harry Emerson Fosdick, speaking at a dinner given by John D. Rockefeller Jr. to introduce Alcoholics Anonymous:

"I think that psychologically speaking there is a point of advantage in the approach that is being made in this movement that cannot be duplicated. I suspect that if it is wisely handled—and it seems to be in wise and prudent hands—there are doors of opportunity ahead of this project that may surpass our capacities to imagine."

Overview

Added to clarify that spiritual awakenings need not be sudden or dramatic. Most develop gradually - the "educational variety."

📖 Key Terms - Appendix II

Definition: Spiritual Experience Term

A personality change sufficient to bring about recovery from alcoholism. May be sudden or gradual. Characterized by a new awareness of a Power greater than oneself.

"The terms 'spiritual experience' and 'spiritual awakening' are used many times in this book..."

Definition: Spiritual Awakening Term

Used interchangeably with "spiritual experience." The result of working the Twelve Steps. A new consciousness of God's will and power to live by it.

Step 12: "Having had a spiritual awakening as the result of these steps..."

Definition: Educational Variety James's Term

William James's term for spiritual experiences that develop gradually over time, through learning and practice, as opposed to sudden conversions. Most AA members experience this type.

"Most of our experiences are what the psychologist William James calls the 'educational variety'"

Definition: God-Consciousness Religious Term

A religious term for the awareness of God's presence and guidance. Used by more religiously-oriented AA members to describe their spiritual experience. Not required - just one way to describe it.

"Our more religious members call it 'God-consciousness.'"

Definition: Power Greater Than Ourselves Core Concept

Any conception of a power beyond oneself that can restore sanity. May be God, the group, the universe, nature - defined individually by each member. The key is that it's greater than your ego.

"...their own conception of a Power greater than themselves."

Definition: W.H.O. (Willingness, Honesty, Open-Mindedness) Essentials

The three "essentials of recovery" and "indispensable" attitudes. Without these, recovery is not possible. Not passive states but active practices to cultivate daily.

"Willingness, honesty and open mindedness are the essentials of recovery. But these are indispensable."

Definition: Contempt Prior to Investigation Barrier

Rejecting an idea without first examining it. The barrier that keeps closed-minded people "in everlasting ignorance." The antidote is investigation through action.

"That principle is contempt prior to investigation."

Definition: Personality Change Result

The transformation necessary for recovery. Not just behavior modification but a fundamental shift in character, outlook, and reaction to life. Same as "psychic change" (Dr. Silkworth's term).

"...personality change sufficient to bring about recovery from alcoholism..."

Definition: Psychic Change Medical Term

Dr. Silkworth's medical term for what the Big Book calls "spiritual experience." The fundamental mental shift that makes recovery possible. Same transformation, different vocabulary.

Doctor's Opinion p. xxix: "Once a psychic change has occurred, the very same person who seemed doomed... will have no difficulty."

Definition: Daily Reprieve Concept

We are not permanently "cured" but receive a daily stay of execution from alcoholism, contingent on maintaining our spiritual condition. Recovery is a daily practice, not a one-time event.

Big Book p. 85: "What we really have is a daily reprieve contingent on the maintenance of our spiritual condition."

Definition: Fourth Dimension of Existence Spiritual Reality

Bill's term for spiritual reality - beyond physical (1st), mental (2nd), and emotional (3rd) dimensions. The realm accessed through spiritual awakening. Most enter gradually, not dramatically.

Big Book p. 25: "We have been rocketed into a fourth dimension of existence of which we had not even dreamed."

"Spiritual Experience" & "Awakening" Terms

These terms are used interchangeably throughout the Big Book. Both refer to a personality change sufficient to bring about recovery - not necessarily a religious conversion.

Step 12: "Having had a spiritual awakening as the result of these steps..."

Personality Change Sufficient Definition

The Big Book defines spiritual experience functionally: it's a change sufficient to bring about recovery. The measure isn't the experience itself, but whether it works - does the person stay sober and live differently?

Point to Ponder: Have you experienced a personality change? Do others notice the difference?

Sudden & Spectacular Misconception

The first printing of the Big Book featured dramatic stories (Bill's "hot flash," etc.) that led some to believe recovery required a sudden, overwhelming experience. This appendix corrects that misconception.

Happily... Erroneous Relief

These words offer immense relief to those who haven't had a "burning bush" moment. The founders wanted no one excluded because their experience didn't match Bill's dramatic white-light experience.

Revolutionary Changes Examples

Bill W.'s experience (Chapter 1), the violent atheist in "We Agnostics," and others describe sudden transformations. These are real but not required. They are examples, not prescriptions.

Immediate God-Consciousness False Standard

Some newcomers believe they must have an "overwhelming" spiritual experience to recover. This sets an impossible standard that can lead to discouragement and relapse. The program works gradually for most.

By No Means the Rule Statistics

Even among thousands of members, sudden transformations are "frequent" but not typical. The majority experience gradual change. Both paths lead to the same destination: recovery.

William James Psychologist

William James (1842-1910) was a pioneering American psychologist and philosopher. His book "The Varieties of Religious Experience" (1902) profoundly influenced Bill W. and early AA thinking about spiritual transformation.

Educational Variety James's Term

William James distinguished between sudden conversions and gradual ones that develop through "education" - learning, practicing, and growing over time. Most AA members experience this slower transformation.

The "daily reprieve" concept: spiritual growth happens day by day, meeting by meeting, step by step.

Develop Slowly Process

Recovery is a process, not an event. The personality change unfolds over months and years. Looking back, members often can't identify a single moment of change - it accumulated gradually.

Friends Notice First Observation

Others often see our changes before we do. The sponsor, family members, or meeting friends may recognize growth that the recovering person hasn't yet noticed in themselves. This is why fellowship matters.

Step 10: "Continued to take personal inventory" - regular self-examination helps us see our own progress.

Profound Alteration Result

"Profound alteration in his reaction to life" - not just abstinence from alcohol, but a fundamentally different way of responding to life's situations. The problem was never just drinking; it was living.

Months vs. Years Evidence

What the program accomplishes in months would take years of self-discipline alone - if it worked at all. This is evidence that something more than willpower is operating: a Power greater than ourselves.

Unsuspected Inner Resource Discovery

Members discover something within themselves they didn't know was there. This "inner resource" connects them to their Higher Power. It was always present but blocked by alcoholism and ego.

Step 11: "Sought through prayer and meditation to improve our conscious contact" - developing this resource.

Power Greater Than Themselves Core Concept

The heart of AA's solution: connecting with a Power greater than oneself. This Power is identified individually - "their own conception" - making AA spiritually inclusive rather than religiously exclusive.

Essence of Spiritual Experience Definition

The Big Book offers a working definition: awareness of a Power greater than ourselves IS the spiritual experience. Not a feeling, not a vision - simply an awareness that changes how we live.

God-Consciousness Religious Term

More religious members may use traditional language like "God-consciousness." The program accommodates both religious and non-religious language for the same spiritual reality.

Any Alcoholic Can Recover Promise

"Most emphatically" - the founders are emphatic about this promise. ANY alcoholic who honestly faces their problems can recover. This is not a program for the few or the special; it's available to all.

Close His Mind Barrier

The only disqualification: closing one's mind to spiritual concepts entirely. Not belief, but openness is required. An atheist who is open can recover; a believer who is closed cannot.

Intolerance or Belligerent Denial Obstacles

Two attitudes that defeat recovery: (1) Intolerance - refusing to consider others' spiritual views; (2) Belligerent denial - aggressively rejecting all spiritual concepts. These are ego-driven positions.

No One Need Have Difficulty Accessibility

The spirituality of the program is designed to be accessible to everyone. It requires no specific beliefs, no church membership, no theological education - just willingness, honesty, and open-mindedness.

W.H.O. - The Essentials Acronym

Willingness, Honesty, Open-mindedness - "the essentials of recovery." Some use "HOW" (Honesty, Open-mindedness, Willingness). Either way, these three qualities are the foundation of recovery.

Point to Ponder: Which of the three - willingness, honesty, or open-mindedness - do you find most challenging?

Indispensable Non-Negotiable

"But these are indispensable." Not suggestions, not recommendations - indispensable. Without willingness, honesty, and open-mindedness, the program cannot work. These are prerequisites.

Contempt Prior to Investigation Famous Quote

This quote warns against dismissing ideas before examining them. The alcoholic who says "AA won't work" or "I don't believe in God" without investigation remains in "everlasting ignorance." Try it first.

Note: This quote is often misattributed to Herbert Spencer. It actually comes from William Paley's writings, though the exact source is disputed.

Herbert Spencer Attribution

Herbert Spencer (1820-1903) was an English philosopher and sociologist. While this quote is attributed to him in AA literature, its actual origin is uncertain. The wisdom remains valid regardless of source.

Open-mindedness is the antidote to "contempt prior to investigation." The Big Book warns against prejudice: "We had to fearlessly face the proposition that either God is everything or else He is nothing" (p. 53).

"Investigation" means actually trying the program - attending meetings, getting a sponsor, working the Steps. Reading about AA or thinking about it isn't investigation; doing it is.

Big Book p. 46: "As soon as we admitted the possible existence of a Creative Intelligence, a Spirit of the Universe underlying the totality of things, we began to be possessed of a new sense of power and direction."

Evidence of Personality Change Signs

How do you know if personality change has occurred? Look for: (1) Reduced selfishness; (2) Increased concern for others; (3) Ability to handle situations that used to trigger drinking; (4) Inner peace; (5) Improved relationships.

Big Book p. 84: "We will not regret the past nor wish to shut the door on it... We will comprehend the word serenity and we will know peace."

Spiritual, Not Religious Distinction

AA is spiritual, not religious. Religion is organized, doctrinal, institutional. Spirituality is personal, experiential, individual. AA has no creed, no dogma, no clergy - just principles and practices that work.

Big Book p. 28: "If a mere code of morals or a better philosophy of life were sufficient to overcome alcoholism, many of us would have recovered long ago."
Tradition 3: The only requirement for membership is a desire to stop drinking. No religious requirements whatsoever.

Sponsor Study Questions ▼
Q47 Why is it important for a newcomer to understand that spiritual experiences don't have to be sudden and dramatic?
So they don't get discouraged waiting for a "burning bush" moment. Most recovery happens gradually. If they understand this, they won't give up because they haven't had Bill's "white light" experience.
Q48 How can the medical endorsements in Appendix III help a skeptical newcomer?
It shows that respected doctors and psychiatrists validated AA's approach. For someone who thinks AA is "just religion" or "unscientific," these endorsements demonstrate that medical professionals found AA effective.

Willingness: being ready to try something different. Honesty: facing the truth about yourself, even when it hurts. Open-mindedness: not dismissing spiritual concepts before trying them. These are prerequisites for the Steps to work.

Examples: "I don't need meetings," "The God stuff won't work for me," "I'm different from other alcoholics." Address it by encouraging them to try the program fully before judging it - attend 90 meetings in 90 days, get a sponsor, work the Steps, then decide.
Advanced: Paradoxes & Cross-References ▼
Q51 What is the paradox of "surrender to win" and how does Appendix II illustrate it?
The paradox: we couldn't change ourselves through effort, but change happened when we stopped trying to control and surrendered. The appendix notes that what takes "months" through surrender couldn't be achieved through "years of self-discipline" alone. Giving up the fight is how we win.
Q52 How does "helping yourself by helping others" (Appendix V) connect to Step 12?
The Living Church editorial states "a man cannot help himself except by helping others." Step 12 says "we tried to carry this message to alcoholics." Both express the same principle: service to others is essential to our own recovery. It's "self-insurance."
Q53 How does Appendix II connect to "The Doctor's Opinion"?
The Doctor's Opinion introduces the need for a "psychic change" (medical term). Appendix II explains what that psychic change looks like: a "spiritual experience" or "spiritual awakening." Same concept, different vocabulary - medical vs. spiritual language for the same transformation.
Q54 What's the connection between Appendix II and Chapter 4 "We Agnostics"?
Both address the question: "Can skeptics and non-believers recover?" Both answer yes. Chapter 4 shows how agnostics came to believe; Appendix II emphasizes that belief isn't required - only "willingness, honesty, and open-mindedness." Openness, not faith, is the key.
Q55 How do the Promises (pp. 83-84) relate to the "profound alteration" mentioned in Appendix II?
The Promises describe the results of spiritual awakening: "new freedom," "serenity," "fear of people and economic insecurity will leave." Appendix II's "profound alteration in reaction to life" IS what the Promises describe. The Promises are the spiritual awakening made concrete.
Q56 What AA Tradition is reflected in "no crusaders; not a temperance society"?
Tradition 10: "AA has no opinion on outside issues." AA doesn't campaign against alcohol, support prohibition, or engage in social crusades. Our only purpose is helping alcoholics recover. This focus is why AA survives while temperance movements faded.
Q57 Why are doctors named in Appendix III but AA members remain anonymous?
Traditions 11 and 12 require anonymity at the public level for AA members. Doctors endorsing AA aren't bound by AA traditions - they're speaking professionally. This illustrates how AA cooperates with professionals while maintaining its own principles of anonymity.
Q58 What analogy does Dr. Kennedy use for AA, and what does it reveal about how AA works?
He calls AA a "therapeutic weapon" that draws on "two greatest reservoirs of power: religion and the herd instinct." This reveals AA's dual power: spiritual principles PLUS fellowship. Neither alone is sufficient - AA combines both for its unique effectiveness.
Q59 What warning does the Herbert Spencer quote give, and how should newcomers respond?
Warning: "Contempt prior to investigation" keeps people in "everlasting ignorance." Response: Don't dismiss AA without trying it. Commit to thorough investigation - 90 meetings, sponsor, Steps, Big Book study - THEN form conclusions based on experience, not prejudice.
Q60 What contrast does Appendix II draw between sudden and gradual spiritual experiences?
Both are valid paths to the same destination. Sudden experiences (like Bill's "white light") are "frequent but by no means the rule." Most members have "educational variety" experiences that "develop slowly over time." The test isn't the type of experience but whether recovery results.

Key Concepts Summary ▼

Spiritual Experience Defined

A spiritual experience is not a feeling or a vision - it is a personality change sufficient to bring about recovery. The measure is practical: does it work? If you've changed enough to stay sober and live differently, you've had a spiritual experience.

Two Paths to Awakening

Spiritual awakenings come in two varieties: (1) Sudden - dramatic, immediate transformations like Bill's "white light" experience; (2) Gradual - the "educational variety" that develops slowly over time. Both are valid; most members experience the gradual type.

The W.H.O. Formula

Willingness, Honesty, Open-mindedness - the three "essentials of recovery" that are "indispensable." These attitudes, not beliefs, are required. An atheist with these qualities can recover; a believer without them cannot.

The Two Reservoirs of Power

AA taps two fundamental human needs: (1) Spirituality - connection to something greater than ourselves; (2) Fellowship - the "herd instinct," our need to belong. This combination is unique and powerful.

Alcoholism as Illness

The Lasker Award citation praised AA for "emphasizing alcoholism as an illness," helping remove moral stigma. This shift from "bad person" to "sick person" revolutionized how alcoholics could be helped.

Self-Insurance Principle

Working with others "insures" our own sobriety. This is why Step 12 work is not optional charity but essential to staying sober. We keep what we have by giving it away.

The Kinship of Common Suffering

AA's therapy is based on shared experience - "the kinship of common suffering." Alcoholics understand each other in ways non-alcoholics cannot. This mutual identification creates healing connection.

Ego Deflation

Dr. Tiebout identified the need to break down "inner resistance" before recovery can begin. The alcoholic's ego, denial, and self-will must be deflated. Only then can spiritual growth occur.

AA and Faith Traditions

Religious leaders across denominations have endorsed AA. It doesn't compete with religion - it complements it. "Poor Catholics become better Catholics," as Father Dowling noted. AA strengthens, rather than replaces, religious practice.

The AA Model's Legacy

The Lasker citation predicted AA's "vast potential for the myriad other ills of mankind." This came true: the 12-step model has been adapted for narcotics addiction, overeating, gambling, codependency, and many other conditions worldwide.

The Only Barrier

The appendix identifies only ONE thing that can defeat the recovering alcoholic: "an attitude of intolerance or belligerent denial." Not lack of education, not severity of alcoholism, not circumstances - only a closed mind blocks recovery.

AA's Core Paradoxes

Recovery is built on paradox: (1) Surrender to win - we stop fighting and find victory; (2) Give it away to keep it - we stay sober by helping others; (3) Powerless yet powerful - admitting weakness unlocks strength. These paradoxes aren't riddles; they're spiritual truths that experience validates.

Recovery Requires Action

The essentials (W.H.O.) are not passive states but active practices. Be WILLING to try. Be HONEST every day. Stay OPEN to new understanding. "Investigation" means doing, not just thinking. The spiritual experience comes through working the Steps, not contemplating them.

Traditions Foreshadowed

The appendices (written before the Traditions) already contain their seeds: Autonomy (AA is "a group unto themselves"), Non-professionalism ("best results under their own guidance"), No outside issues ("no crusaders; not a temperance society"), Anonymity (doctors named, AA members not).

Metaphors That Teach

The appendices use powerful analogies: Allergy (physical reaction to alcohol), Self-insurance (helping others protects us), Therapeutic weapon (AA fights the disease). These metaphors make abstract concepts concrete and memorable.

Key Contrasts

Understanding comes through comparison: Sudden vs. gradual awakening (both valid), Religious vs. spiritual (AA is the latter), Medical vs. spiritual solution (both needed, spiritual is primary). These contrasts clarify what AA is and isn't.

Study Assignments

The appendices point to additional resources: William James ("The Varieties of Religious Experience"), "Three Talks to Medical Societies" pamphlet. These instruction markers guide deeper study beyond the Big Book itself.

Everything Connects

The appendices don't stand alone. They connect to: Doctor's Opinion (psychic change = spiritual awakening), We Agnostics (openness, not belief), The Promises (what awakening looks like), Step 12 ("as the result of these steps"). Study the connections.

Don't overcomplicate recovery. The program is simple (though not easy): Don't drink, go to meetings, work the Steps, help others.

Slogan: Easy Does It Patience

Take it slow. Don't rush recovery. Avoid the perfectionism that can lead to frustration and relapse. Progress, not perfection.

Big Book p. 60: "No one among us has been able to maintain anything like perfect adherence to these principles."

Slogan: First Things First Priority

Sobriety comes first. Without sobriety, nothing else matters. Prioritize recovery above other concerns - job, relationships, everything.

Slogan: Let Go and Let God Surrender

Stop trying to control everything. Turn outcomes over to your Higher Power. Relates to Step 3 (turning will and life over to God's care).

Big Book p. 63: "The alcoholic is an extreme example of self-will run riot."
📖 Types of Meetings

Meeting: Big Book Study Format

Meeting focused on reading and discussing the Big Book. Members take turns reading paragraphs and share what the text means to them. These appendices are often studied here.

Meeting: Step Study Format

Meeting focusing on one or more of the 12 Steps. May use Big Book, 12&12, or other Conference-approved literature. Appendix II is especially relevant for Step 12.

Meeting: Speaker Meeting Format

Meeting where members share their story (what it was like, what happened, what it's like now). Demonstrates the educational variety of spiritual experience described in Appendix II.
